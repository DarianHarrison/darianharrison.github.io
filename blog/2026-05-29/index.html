A Framework for Sustainable Operations

Demand, Supply, and Eco-Societal Operations.

Darian Harrison published on May 29, 2026
7 min, 1276 words

Figure 1: The Sustainable Operations Ecosystem. A conceptual architecture illustrating the interdependencies of operational sustainability. The three primary pillars: Demand Operations, Supply Operations, and Eco-Societal Operations, overlap to forge integrated execution frameworks like Target Product Profiles (TPP) and Concurrent TEA-LCA. A central, anchored core of operational governance (QMS) maintains the ecosystem's structural integrity, allowing sustainable operations to scale smoothly.

Over the years, my perspective on the concept of "sustainability" has evolved significantly. I have come to believe that true sustainability is an operational ecosystem that relies on three distinct, interdependent pillars:

  1. Demand: Validating market viability and ensuring the fulfillment of actual societal needs.
  2. Supply: The mechanics of designing, producing, and scaling that value efficiently.
  3. Eco-Society: Managing our shared ecological footprint and integrating ethical responsibility across the value chain.

When an organization holds itself accountable to all three operational pillars simultaneously, it establishes a foundation for long-term flourishing. The following sections provide an objective structural breakdown of these pillars. To ground these high-level concepts in concrete mechanics, this article utilizes industrial biomanufacturing to illustrate how minimal operational standards support the overarching ecosystem.

1. Demand Operations: Validating Market Value

Demand operations focus on economic reality. If a product, service, or technology does not deliver genuine utility to the end-user, all resources utilized to create it are fundamentally wasted.

Commercializing high technologies is inherently expensive and fraught with market uncertainty. To achieve market viability, organizations must deploy structured commercialization frameworks that seek to validate demand and feasibility early, preventing the misallocation of capital.

The Target Product Profile (TPP) and Resource Allocation

A critical metric for this pillar is the health of the New Product Development (NPD) pipeline. Organizations frequently suffer from pipeline bloat due to "Zombie Projects"—initiatives that formally pass early approval gates but lack the backed resource commitments to support execution. They consume administrative bandwidth and block higher-value commercial initiatives.

Biomanufacturing Application: To maintain resilient demand operations, biopharma and biotech organizations utilize a Target Product Profile (TPP). A TPP is a strategic framework established at inception that mathematically defines the minimal and ideal commercial criteria (efficacy, cost, target population, shelf-life) a product must achieve to be economically viable. By pairing a rigid TPP with in-silico AI modeling—which validates therapeutic targets or the feasibility of alternative proteins in the cloud before committing a single drop of wet-lab reagent—organizations create an absolute resource gate, preventing massive capital sinks in later clinical phases.

2. Supply Operations: The Mechanics of Production

Supply operations dictate the physical execution of manufacturing. This pillar ensures that internal operations are continuously optimized to eliminate defects, maximize yield, and reduce material consumption.

This is structurally achieved through frameworks such as Green Lean Six Sigma (GLSS), which incorporates eco-friendly objectives directly into traditional process optimization.

Defect Eradication and the "Hidden Factory"

In production, efficiency is measured by exposing the "Hidden Factory"—the unrecorded capacity a plant consumes correcting its own internal mistakes. Traditional metrics like First Pass Yield (FPY) often hide this wasted capacity. Instead, operations must rely on Rolled Throughput Yield (RTY), measuring the systemic probability of a unit passing through the entire multi-step process without any rework loops.

Biomanufacturing Application: Continuous Processing & RTRT

In biomanufacturing, operations span from upstream processing (USP)—cell culture and fermentation—to downstream processing (DSP)—molecular purification. To achieve supply efficiency across both phases, the industry is transitioning toward continuous processing enabled by Digital Twins.

Utilizing Process Analytical Technology (PAT), in-line sensors feed continuous data directly into predictive models, triggering autonomous, closed-loop adjustments to bioreactors. The ultimate operational milestone here is Real-Time Release Testing (RTRT). By continuously proving the product meets quality specifications during the run via PAT, organizations can eliminate traditional 30-day Quality Control holding periods, simultaneously preventing batch waste and fundamentally accelerating cash cycles.

3. Eco-Societal Operations: The Shared Baseline

The third pillar recognizes that manufacturing exists within a finite natural environment and a shared social fabric. Today, operations require organizations to objectively measure their external footprint and actively manage ethical responsibility across their value chains.

Double Materiality and Concurrent TEA-LCA

Modern sustainability operations are dictated by Double Materiality, a standard mandated by global frameworks like the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). Double Materiality requires organizations to assess two distinct vectors:

  1. Financial Materiality (Inward): How sustainability risks (e.g., climate change, supply chain disruptions) impact the company's enterprise value.
  2. Impact Materiality (Outward): How the company's operations externally impact the environment, specifically tracing Scope 3 Emissions across the upstream and downstream value chain.

To effectively evaluate these inward and outward vectors simultaneously, modern operations cannot silo cost analysis from environmental impact. The critical operational enabler here is Concurrent TEA-LCA (Techno-Economic Assessment combined with Life Cycle Assessment). Optimizing purely for cost (TEA) might inadvertently increase the carbon footprint (LCA), and vice versa. Evaluating them concurrently ensures the physical supply mechanics and the environmental footprint are optimized simultaneously.

Biomanufacturing Application: Second-Generation Feedstocks

To support a positive eco-societal impact, the bioeconomy is leveraging TEA-LCA models to shift away from "first-generation" feedstocks (edible crops like corn), which drive deforestation and compete with global food security. Sustainable biotechnology focuses instead on "second-generation" feedstocks—recalcitrant lignocellulosic biomass derived from non-edible agricultural waste. Standardizing this constraint forces innovation in enzymatic depolymerization, ultimately actualizing a circular bioeconomy without sacrificing process economics.

4. The Enabling Backbone: Minimal Operational Standards

For these three operations to function synergistically, they must be supported by a subtle baseline of governance. The objective is to deploy adaptive frameworks that clarify operational boundaries without stifling ground-level execution.

A primary example is the formalization of cross-functional decision rights using frameworks like RAPID (Recommend, Agree, Perform, Input, Decide). There is only ever one designated "Decide" role to prevent deadlocks. For highly regulated industries like biotechnology, this governance must be formally embedded within the organization’s Quality Management System (QMS) to ensure regulatory compliance is maintained alongside operational agility.

Conclusion

Treating sustainability as an integrated ecosystem—balancing Demand Operations, Supply Operations, and Eco-Societal Operations—provides a highly resilient blueprint for scaling.

By applying a minimal layer of operational standards, an organization can avoid bureaucratic overreach while preventing chaotic operational drift. Aligning key performance indicators with physical, market, and ethical realities ensures the ecosystem is well-regulated. Ultimately, when these constraints are consistently respected, they cease to be limitations and become the primary catalysts for sustainable innovation.
